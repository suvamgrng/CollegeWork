What is Linear Programming?
Understanding the 3 building blocks before anything else
The Big Idea

LP is a mathematical method to find the best possible decision (max profit / min cost) given limited resources. Think of it like this:

Real-world analogy: You run a factory. You have limited machine hours, labour and raw materials. LP tells you exactly how many of each product to make so you earn the most money — without breaking any limit.

Every LP problem has exactly three parts:

① Decision Variables

The unknowns you are deciding. Usually labelled x and y. They represent the quantities you control.

x = units of Product 1
y = units of Product 2

⚠ Always non-negative: x ≥ 0, y ≥ 0

② Objective Function

The formula for what you want to maximise or minimise. Always linear (no x², no xy).

Maximize Z = 2x + 3y
— or —
Minimize Z = 5x + 8y

Z is the value you are optimising (profit, cost, etc.)

③ Constraints

Resource limits expressed as linear inequalities. They form the "walls" of what is possible.

2x + 4y ≤ 20 (Machine A)
2x + 2y ≤ 12 (Machine B)
4x ≤ 16 (Machine C)

≤ for limits, ≥ for minimum requirements

Types of LP Problems (know all four!)
🏭 Manufacturing Problem
How many of each product to produce to maximise profit with limited machines/labour.
🥗 Diet Problem
Minimise cost of food while meeting minimum nutritional requirements. Uses ≥ constraints.
🚛 Transportation Problem
Minimise shipping costs from factories to warehouses with supply/demand limits.
📋 Assignment Problem
Assign workers/machines to jobs to minimise total cost or time.
How to Formulate an LPP
4 steps to convert a word problem into mathematics
1
Identify Decision Variables

Read the problem. What are you deciding "how many of"? Label them x and y. Write a "Let x = …" sentence.

2
Write the Objective Function

What are you maximising or minimising? Usually profit per unit × quantity. Write: Maximize Z = … or Minimize Z = …

3
Express Each Resource Limit as a Constraint

For each limited resource: (usage per unit of x)×x + (usage per unit of y)×y ≤ available amount. Use ≥ for minimum requirements.

4
Add Non-negativity Constraints

Always write: x ≥ 0, y ≥ 0 at the end. You cannot produce negative quantities!

Worked Formulation: Watch Dealer (Example 1 from slides)

M1 costs $100 (profit $20), M2 costs $200 (profit $50). Max 30 watches, max $4000 budget.

STEP 1 – Variables
Let x = No. of M1 watches
Let y = No. of M2 watches
STEP 2 – Objective Function
Maximize Z = 20x + 50y
STEP 3 – Constraints
x + y ≤ 30 ← showcase limit (at most 30 watches total)
100x + 200y ≤ 4000 ← budget limit ($4000 max)
STEP 4 – Non-negativity
x ≥ 0, y ≥ 0

The Graphical Method — Step by Step
Used when there are exactly 2 decision variables (x and y)
1
Formulate the LPP

Write the objective function and all constraints. (You already know how to do this from Tab 2!)

2
Convert inequalities → equalities for graphing

Replace ≤ or ≥ with = to get straight line equations you can plot. E.g., 2x + 4y ≤ 20 becomes 2x + 4y = 20.

3
Find two points on each line and plot

Put x = 0 to find y-intercept, then put y = 0 to find x-intercept. Draw the line through both points.

4
Do the Origin Test

Substitute (0,0) into the original inequality. If it's TRUE → origin side is the feasible region (shade toward origin). If FALSE → shade away from origin.

5
Find corner points of the feasible region

The feasible region is where ALL shaded areas overlap. Find every corner (vertex) by solving pairs of boundary equations simultaneously.

6
Evaluate Z at each corner → conclusion

Plug each corner (x,y) into the objective function Z. The corner giving the highest Z (for max) or lowest Z (for min) is the optimal solution.

Key Concepts: Feasible Region
Bounded (Closed) Region
The feasible region is a closed polygon (like a triangle or quadrilateral). Has a finite maximum AND minimum. Happens when all constraints are ≤.
Unbounded (Open) Region
The feasible region extends infinitely. Can have a finite minimum (for minimisation) but no maximum. Common in diet problems with ≥ constraints.
Corner Point Theorem: The optimal value of Z (if it exists) always occurs at one of the corner points of the feasible region. That's why you only need to check corners — not every point inside!

Problem Statement

A factory has 3 resources: A (20 units), B (12 units), C (16 units). Product 1 needs 2A, 2B, 4C and gives $2 profit. Product 2 needs 4A, 2B, 0C and gives $3 profit. Maximise profit.

Step 1–2: Formulation
Let x = units of Product 1, y = units of Product 2

Maximize Z = 2x + 3y

Subject to:

2x + 2y ≤ 12 … (ii) Resource B

Prakash Electric Bulb Company — 3 plants, 3 models

Plant 1: 8000 Model1, 4000 Model2, 8000 Model3 per day — Cost Rs.55,000/day
Plant 2: 6000 Model1, 6000 Model2, 3000 Model3 per day — Cost Rs.60,000/day

Key insight: Decision variables = days each plant operates (x₁, x₂, x₃). Objective = minimise total cost. Constraints = each model's demand must be MET (so use ≥, not ≤). This makes it a minimisation problem!
